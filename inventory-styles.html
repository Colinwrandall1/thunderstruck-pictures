Inventory · Card treatment study

Four ways to handle the product cards.

Same gear, four card styles, judged against the real source images — which are a mix of clean white-background shots (Nanlux, Astera) and messy lifestyle/marketing images (the Aputure 1200x "BLAIR" shot, the 600d on dark bokeh). The white-vs-dark clash is the thing we're solving. Scroll and compare.

01Refined light cards

Lean into it — soft off-white cards (not stark white), consistent padding, dark text, a yellow edge on hover. The bright catalog plates become a deliberate rhythm against the dark frame. This is the "clean, bright product library" Martin liked at Charlotte Film Rentals.

Best: handles any source image · familiar e-comm feel  |  Cost: brightest option, least cinematic

02Contained plate on dark

Card stays dark to match the page; the photo sits in a smaller rounded light "plate" with the name, spec, and price on the dark area below. Shrinks the bright footprint so the white reads as an intentional gear swatch, not a glaring box.

Best: cohesive + works with any source image · my pick for "now"  |  Cost: smaller product image

03Duotone on dark

Every image gets a grayscale + contrast + brand-tint treatment so wildly different sources unify into one moody, editorial look — full color blooms back on hover. Most cinematic, very on-brand with the storm.

Best: unifies lifestyle/varied shots beautifully  |  Cost: pure white backgrounds still read light (see Nanlux) · loses true product color

04Knockout on dark (the end-state)

Background-removed product floating on a dark card with a soft spotlight + shadow — the B&H / Aputure-premium look, the most cohesive of all. Shown here with the items whose source photos have clean white backgrounds (I knocked them out for this mock).

Best: most premium + cohesive  |  Needs: clean product photography — exactly what Martin's planned gear shoot can deliver (shoot every item on a seamless)

05Stormfront

The one you spotted — knockout products floating over slowly rolling storm clouds, in translucent cards with viewfinder corner-brackets and a soft reflection. The cloud drift is live (give it a few seconds). This is the most "Thunderstruck" of the bunch — the brand baked into the card itself.

Best: unmistakably on-brand, premium, cinematic  |  Needs: clean cutouts (same as #4) · heavier visually, best for a "Featured" row rather than a 50-item catalog

Same data, four treatments. My recommendation: #2 (contained plate) or #3 (duotone) to ship now, moving to #4 (knockouts) once Martin shoots gear on a seamless.
